# Brand Identity Reference

This document provides a technical reference for visual and verbal brand identity components,
rhetorical devices, grammatical checklists, and industry case studies.

---

## 1. Visual Identity Components

The visual identity is a system of design assets that express the brand strategy visually.

- **Logo Types**:
  - _Wordmark (Logotype)_: A text-only representation of the brand name using custom typography
    (e.g., Google, Bolt).
  - _Symbol (Isotype)_: An abstract or pictorial icon that represents the brand without text
    (e.g., Apple's apple, Nike's swoosh).
  - _Combination Mark (Imagotype)_: A layout featuring both the wordmark and the symbol together.
- **Color Palette Categories**:
  - _Primary Colors_: The dominant colors that represent the brand on 60% of assets.
  - _Secondary Colors_: Supporting shades that provide depth and visual interest on 30% of assets.
  - _Accent Colors_: High-contrast shades used exclusively for focal points and CTAs (10% of assets).
- **Typography Hierarchy**:
  - _Display/Heading Serif or Sans-Serif_: Sets the stylistic tone. Used at large scale.
  - _Body Sans-Serif_: A neutral typeface optimized for readability on mobile and desktop screens.
  - _System Font Fallbacks_: Standard OS web-safe fonts used to ensure fast page load times.
- **Imagery and Graphics**:
  - _Vector Icons_: Clean, flat icons built on a shared stroke weight grid.
  - _Photography Guidelines_: Specifications for lighting, depth of field, and authentic settings.

## 2. Verbal Identity Components

The verbal identity is the structured system of language used to express the brand personality.

- **Tone of Voice Attributes**:
  - Defined by specific adjectives (e.g., dry, plainspoken, comforting).
  - Accompanied by usage principles and structural limitations ("X, but not Y").
- **Strategic Statements**:
  - _Mission Statement_: Communicates the current value proposition (the what, for whom, and how).
  - _Vision Statement_: Communicates the long-term aspirational destination (the where).
  - _Purpose Statement_: Communicates the fundamental reason for existence beyond commercial gain
    (the why).
- **Tagline (Slogan)**:
  - A memorable phrase that sums up the brand's mission or differentiator (e.g., Nike's "Just do it").

## 3. Brand Messaging Framework

The Messaging Framework structures how a brand communicates its value proposition across different
marketing platforms and user segments.

| Component                 | Target Audience       | Focus Area              | Example Structure                 |
| :------------------------ | :-------------------- | :---------------------- | :-------------------------------- |
| **Elevator Pitch**        | Investors / New users | Quick value hook        | "We help X do Y by automating Z." |
| **Pillar 1: Innovation**  | Industry experts      | Product capabilities    | Focus on technical features.      |
| **Pillar 2: Reliability** | Enterprise clients    | Service uptime / Safety | Focus on SLAs and guarantees.     |
| **Pillar 3: Human Touch** | End users             | Ease of use / Support   | Focus on UX and onboarding.       |

## 4. Rhetorical Devices for Verbal Identity

Utilize these standard rhetorical devices to increase copy memorability and rhythm:

- **Alliteration**: The repetition of initial consonant sounds in neighboring words.
  - _Example_: "Shockingly simple shopping" (Bolt).
- **Tricolon**: A series of three parallel words, phrases, or clauses.
  - _Example_: "Browse. Click. Buy." or "Simple, fast, secure."
- **Antithesis**: The juxtaposition of contrasting ideas in balanced phrases.
  - _Example_: "Password hint: Forget it." or "Less buzz, more biz."
- **Metaphor**: An figure of speech that describes an object or action in a way that isn't literally
  true, but helps explain an idea.
  - _Example_: "Lightning-fast checkout" or "AI is the engine, not the story."

## 5. Brand Grammar Checklist

Verify that written content complies with these baseline formatting rules:

- **Abbreviations**:
  - Write out unfamiliar acronyms on the first reference (e.g., "SaaS (Software as a Service)").
- **Capitalization Rules**:
  - _Titles/Headers_: Standardize on Title Case or Sentence Case. Do not mix them in a single view.
  - _UI Buttons_: Always use Sentence Case (e.g., "Start your free trial").
- **Numeric Phrasing**:
  - Spell out numbers from one to nine (e.g., "three features").
  - Use digits for numbers 10 and above (e.g., "15 integrations").
- **Regional Localization**:
  - Select either US English (e.g., _color_, _analyze_) or UK English (e.g., _colour_, _analyse_)
    and stick to it across all assets.

## 6. Industry Case Studies Reference

Use these established brand strategies as design and communication models:

### Apple Inc. (Visual & Verbal Simplicity)

- **Visuals**: Earthy/monochromatic scales, high white space ratios, minimal typography, crisp photos.
- **Verbal**: Direct, active voice, minimal technical jargon, focus on product design.
- **Rhetorical Style**: Focus on innovation and clean typography.

### Mailchimp (B2B Conversational Humor)

- **Visuals**: Quirky hand-drawn illustrations, warm primary colors, playful bold typefaces.
- **Verbal**: Plainspoken, genuine, translators of complex ideas with dry, subtle humor.
- **Rhetorical Style**: Friendly, welcoming, avoids jargon.

### Bolt (Direct Technical Impact)

- **Visuals**: Structured dark layouts, high-contrast accents, custom checkout buttons.
- **Verbal**: Thoughtfully concise, comfortably at ease, minimal tech talk.
- **Rhetorical Style**: Heavy use of tricolons and antithesis to emphasize speed.

### Vidext (Empathetic AI Tools)

- **Visuals**: Flat illustrations, clean geometric UI mockups, clear layout grids.
- **Verbal**: Clarity, confidence, human touch, focus on ownership and freedom.
- **Rhetorical Style**: Employs contrasts and user-benefit statements to reassure content creators.
